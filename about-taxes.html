Canadian businesses are burdened with the payment of income taxes, sales taxes, employee taxes, excise taxes and capital taxes. We offer a wide range of taxation services for both personal and corporate tax clients.

INCOME TAXES

The hardest thing in the world to understand is the income tax.
Albert Einstein

Eventually, we all have to deal with income taxes. Income tax is collected by the CRA – Canada Customs and Revenue Agency - on behalf of the federal government, provinces and territories. Tax return consists of the reporting the sum of the previous year's taxable income, tax credits, and other information relating to those two items. The result of filing a return with the federal government can result in either a refund (money owed to the person or corporation filing the return), or an amount due to be paid.

Personal/Individual Tax Returns (T1)

  • personal tax returns

    • Preparation and filing of a personal tax return (T1) must be completed annually and submitted to CRA no later than April 30th of the following year. If you have a balance owing, it must be paid by April 30th. If you, your spouse or common-law partner carried on a business during the tax year, your return has to be filed by June 15th. However, if you have a balance owing, it still must be paid by April 30th.

  • tax returns for sole proprietors, partnerships

    • Income or losses from an unincorporated business are reported on the personal tax returns of the proprietor or partners for the calendar year in which the business year end falls into. The amount of tax will depend on other personal sources of income or deductions. These personal tax rates in Ontario range from 21 % to 50% depending on your taxable income.

Corporate Tax Returns (T2)

  • preparation of financial statements in order to submit the corporate tax return with information supplied by the client and filing of the Canadian Corporate Tax Return (T2).

    • The year-end can be either the fiscal year end of the business or December 31. Most businesses have elected to use their fiscal year end. The corporate income tax return is required to be filed no later than six months following the year end. A penalty is assessed based on the taxes owing for late filed returns. If you are required to make installment payments throughout the year, they are due on March 15, June 15, September 15, and December 15.

    The rate of tax on corporate income depends on the type of business as well as the amount of income. Most active small business corporations will pay tax of 15.5% on the first $500,000 of taxable income and 28.25% for income in excess of $500,000. Corporations that do not earn active business income (i.e. rental or investment companies) pay corporate taxes at approximately 46% on all taxable income other than capital gains. Capital gains are taxed at 23%.

Harmonized Sales Tax (HST)

If you run a business in Canada or provide sales/services in Canada and your turnover for the last four quarters exceeds CAD $30,000, you must register for GST/HST unless the sales are categorized as exempt (such as health care, residential rentals and insurance). Even if you are not required to register your business you may choose to do so in order to claim back any GST/HST that you pay out on your purchases or expenses. When you register for GST/HST, you will receive a business number (BN) - you will need to display this number on your invoices. The annual taxable sales amount will determine what filing options are available to you:

  • Greater than $6,000,000

    • must file monthly, due by the end of the following month.

  • From $1,500,000 to $6,000,000

    • must file at least quarterly but can elect to file monthly. Quarterly returns are due one month following the end of the quarter. The monthly filing option would be used if you were expecting refunds regularly.

  • Under $1,500,000

    • will file quarterly unless an election is made to file monthly or annually. If annual filing is elected then quarterly instalments may be required and the annual return is due three months following the year end. Many small businesses find that the annual filing option suits them best. You may also file your HST using the Simplified Method. You should consult with a qualified accountant to determine which method best suits your business needs.

Source deductions

When you pay remuneration, such as salary or wages, or give a taxable benefit to a recipient, you have to take source deductions from that amount. You then have to remit these deductions to the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA).

The source deductions you have to withhold and remit to the CRA may be any combination of:

  • Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions
  • employment insurance (EI) premiums
  • federal, provincial, or territorial income tax

Payment of the source deductions must reach Canada Revenue Agency by the due date or a penalty could be assessed. Many businesses make their payments at a bank by the 15th of the month in order to comply.

You need to tell the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) if you have no source deductions to remit for a month or quarter.

This can happen if

  • you have no employees
  • you have seasonal workers

Do not report a nil remittance if you made source deductions in the month or quarter.

Report the income and deductions on the T4 slips that you will send to the CRA; give employees their T4 slips and file the T4 Summary, together with the related T4 slips, on or before the last day of February following the calendar year to which the slips apply. Keep your paper and electronic records for six years after the year to which they relate.

As an employer, you will have to know the employment standards and pay equity laws. Also, you will be required to register for Employer Health Tax and Workplace Safety and Insurance.
